Retelling Dubliners

A Walking Tour through Dublin ~ 2 hours

Walk in the footsteps of James Joyce's Dubliners while learning Dublin's iconic history. Starting at the James Joyce Centre and ending at Kennedy's Pub, visit the famous locations mentioned in the book at your own pace.

Navigating Dublin

Frequently asked questions about Dublin's public transport system.

01 What are the main types of public transport in Dublin Centre?

Dublin's public transport network includes buses, trams, trains, and bike sharing schemes. Routes, maps, planning a journey, ticketing, real-time traffic and travel updates can all be found at TransportforIreland.


DublinBikes is a self-service rental system open to everyone aged 14 and up. Users must purchase a ticket (1-day, 3-day, or annual subscription) to access the service. Within each ticket, the first 30 minutes of every individual journey are free. You can view nearby stations and available bikes via the official RedClick dublinbikes app or at a station terminal. Payment is via TFI Leap Card or a dublinbikes card. Bleeper and MOBY Bikes are two stationless bike sharing services also operating in Dublin, mainly in areas not covered by DublinBikes.


Dublin Bus serves Dublin and the Greater Dublin Area, covering the broadest area among all. Services generally run from around 5:30am to 11:30pm, though some 24-hour routes are in operation and Nitelink services are available on weekend nights. You can check out their timetable, or use TFI Live (app version available) to learn about specific operation times for your bus.


Luas (Irish for 'speed') is Dublin's tram network, with two lines serving the city. The Green Line runs north–south through the city centre, while the Red Line connects the southwest to the Docklands. Luas operates on a frequency-based system, not a fixed timetable. You can check real time information for any stop here.


DART is Dublin's electric train system, one of the fastest ways to move through Dublin city centre and out to other parts of the city, running along the coast of the Irish Sea from Malahide or Howth in North County Dublin southwards as far as Greystones, Co. Wicklow. Commuter rail services extend further beyond the DART, reaching into the surrounding counties.


Payment Method: Dublin Bus accepts coins only. Exact fare is required and no change is given. Card and contactless payments are not yet available on board.

02 What apps can I use for transport-related information?

TFI Live is the official Transport for Ireland app. It provides real-time departures for Dublin Bus, Go-Ahead Ireland, Bus Éireann, Luas, and Irish Rail, alongside timetables, interactive maps, and accessibility information including wheelchair-accessible stops and services. You can also save your favourite journeys, stops, and timetables for quick access on the go.

TFI Go App allows you to buy tickets for participating Transport for Ireland bus services and participating commercial bus operators directly from your phone. Your ticket is downloaded to the TFI Go App and can be used to travel straight away.

03 How accessible is Dublin public transport?

Dublin's public transport is generally accessible, though the level of support varies by mode. TFI Journey Planner shows whether your chosen service is wheelchair accessible.

Dublin Bus uses low-floor, wheelchair-accessible buses on all routes. Each bus has one dedicated wheelchair space, accessible via a ramp at the front doors. Luas provides step-free access at all stops and all trams are wheelchair accessible. DART stations are mostly step and gate free, with the exceptions of Greystones, Killiney and Sutton. Wheelchair ramps and staff assistance are available at most stations. If you need to book accessibility assistance for any TFI service, advance notice is required. For full accessibility information across all modes, visit transportforireland.ie/accessibility.

Additionally, Independent Travel Support is a free scheme helping people with disabilities travel confidently across all modes.

04 Is there public transport late at night?

24-hour Routes: Dublin Bus offer fifteen 24-hour services which are in operation seven days a week across ten routes: 15, 39a, 41, C1, C2, C5, C6, E1, E2, G1, G2, N4, F1, F2, 80. Full timetables are available at dublinbus.ie/24-hour-timetables.

Nitelink: Twelve Nitelink bus routes depart from Dublin City Centre every Friday and Saturday night to suburbs all over Dublin: 7n, 29n, 31n, 33n, 42n, 46n, 49n, 69n, 70n, 77n, 84n, 88n. Full timetables are available at dublinbus.ie/nitelink-timetables.

05 How do I get from Dublin Airport to the city?

Dublin Bus (routes 16, 19, 24, 41): Dublin Bus routes 16, 19, 24 and 41 operate between city centre and the airport. You can pay on the bus in coins or by a Leap Card. These are slower but cheaper options.

Dublin Express (routes 782, 783, 784): Dublin Express provides three express routes between Dublin Airport and Dublin City Centre. Tickets can be booked through the Dublin Express website or purchased on board by card only (no cash, no Leap Card). Young Adult and Student Leap Card holders receive a discounted fare.

Aircoach (routes 700 & 700X): The Aircoach service operates up to every 15 minutes between Dublin Airport and Dublin City Centre, continuing onward to Leopardstown. Tickets can be bought on the website, through the Aircoach mobile app, on board, or at the Aircoach departure stand at Terminal 1 (Zone 2) and Terminal 2 (Zone 20). Most services accept both cash and contactless payments on board. Leap Cards are also accepted on Aircoach services for single journey fares.

    The James Joyce Center is a restored Georgian townhouse near the childhood home of James Joyce. Full of exhibitions, readings, lectures, and promoting Joyce's other works. It's a great place to get your bearings and expand your knowledge on the Irish famed author.

    Open from 10:30 AM to 4:30 PM Tuesday through Saturday. Tickets are €7 for adults, €5 for seniors and students (with a valid student ID) and free for children 12 and under.

    Located on North Great George's Street, the James Joyce Center is a restored Georgian townhouse honoring the legacy of James Joyce.

    Full of exhibitions, readings, lectures, and displaying Joyce's other works. It's a great place to get your bearings and expand your knowledge on the famed Irish author.

    Built in 1784 for Valentine Brown, the First Earl of Kenmare, the townhouse was preserved by a Joycean Scholar Senator David Norris in 1982. With the help of generous donors and lovers of Joyce's works, the townhouse was renovated and opened to the public in 1996 as the James Joyce Centre.

    The James Joyce Centre is one of many museums and tributes to Joyce, but features more community centered events. If you happen to visit in February or June, check out their website to see what special events they have to celebrate James Joyce's birthday or the annual Bloomsday Festival.

  2. 2. Trinity College Dublin

    Trinity College Dublin is a historic 400 year old university right in Dublin's city center. Ranked the No. 1 university in Ireland, its hallowed halls have been the home to thought leaders and creative minds, like Oscar Wilde, Bram Stoker, Edmund Burke and more. In Dubliners, Trinity College Dublin appears in three stories, serving both as a central city landmark and as an institution inseparable from Ireland's colonial history.

    "As they passed along the railings of Trinity College, Lenehan skipped out into the road and peered up at the clock" (pg. 53).

    Trinity College Dublin is a historic 400 year old university right in Dublin's city center. Ranked the No. 1 university in Ireland, its hallowed halls have been the home to thought leaders and creative minds, like Oscar Wilde, Bram Stoker, Edmund Burke and more. Trinity College is also home to decades of Joycean Scholars who've spent years studying James Joyce's works.

    In Dubliners, Trinity College Dublin appears in three separate stories, serving both as a central city landmark and as an institution inseparable from Ireland's colonial history.

    "As they passed along the railings of Trinity College, Lenehan skipped out into the road and peered up at the clock" (pg. 53 of Dubliners).

    Founded in 1592, through a royal charter from Queen Elizabeth the First, Trinity is Ireland's oldest university in continuous operation. The university has undergone several changes over the course of its longstanding history but focuses on preservation and honoring the original architecture of the school for its students, staff, and visitors to enjoy.

    Some key reminders while visiting Trinity: as it is an active university campus, please be mindful and courteous to all students and staff while visiting. All buildings with keycard access are restricted to students and staff only. Finally, watch out for the seagulls near the rugby pitch or they'll steal your food.

    For our next stop, leave Trinity's campus through the Nassau Street gate. Then walk up Nassau Street and turn up Wicklow Street to find this literary treasure trove.

    Tucked in between several shops and restaurants on Wicklow Street lies The Secret Book and Record Store, a small, independent bookstore that offers new and secondhand books, CDs and vinyl. Find rare books, vintage vinyl and all the latest releases. Pick up your own copy of Dubliners or any other book by James Joyce for only €5 in their classics collection!

    While browsing the bookstore's selection, here's some history on James Joyce. Born on February 2nd 1882, James Joyce is considered one of Ireland's most celebrated writers for his books, Dubliners and Ulysses. The eldest of 10 children, Joyce lived in Brighton Square, Rathgar with his parents, John Stanislaus Joyce and Mary Jane "May" (née Murray).

    Joyce has led a long storied life, where he wasn't only a writer but an educator as well. His works have been praised by literary figures like T.S. Eliot, James Baldwin and more. Joyce's work has been described as a "colossus of modernist fiction" by the Yale Press. He sought to "give a picture of Dublin so complete that if the city suddenly disappeared from the earth it could be reconstructed out of my book" — from a conversation with Frank Budgen, 1918. For more information on James Joyce's life, visit our website.

  4. 4. City Markets

    Want a souvenir or to do some window shopping, wander through the City Markets on Grafton Street, in the heart of Dublin right between Trinity College and St. Stephen's Green. South City Markets opened in 1881 as Dublin's first purpose-built Victorian shopping arcade, funded by affluent property-owning families and designed by English architects. Today, the market is more known under the name of George's Street Arcade, and has been operating under the Layden Family Group's ownership since 1992.

    In Dubliners, City Market appears in the story "Two Gallants," serving as a turning point directing Lenehan from George's Street toward Grafton Street. More often referred to as the South City Markets, it is a busy covered market complex on South Great George’s Street, associated with small trade, everyday commerce, and a dense pedestrian flow.

    "He left his friends at a quarter to ten and went up George's Street. He turned to the left at the City Markets and walked on into Grafton Street" (pg. 58).

  5. 5. St. Stephen's Green

    A Victorian gem in Dublin's greatest garden square, it's one of Ireland's most famous public parks. This 9-hectare green space has been maintained to its original Victorian layout with over 3.5 km of accessible pathways, a waterfall, ornamental lake and several sculptures; including the James Joyce Memorial Sculpture. St. Stephen's Green appears in "After the Race" and "Two Gallants." Noticeably, it is mentioned three times in "Two Gallants," establishing itself as an important symbolic space in the story. It is described as crowded and noisy, imbued with people, trams, and city lights.

    "That night the city wore the mask of a capital. The five young men strolled along Stephen's Green in a faint cloud of aromatic smoke." (p. 46).

    After you've done some shopping, start making your way down to St. Stephen's Green, a 9 minute walk from the City Markets. A Victorian gem in Dublin's greatest garden square, it's one of Ireland's most famous public parks. This 9-hectare green space has been maintained to its original Victorian layout with over 3.5 km of accessible pathways, a waterfall, ornamental lake and several sculptures — including the James Joyce Memorial Sculpture.

    A little known fact is that this public park used to be marshland that served as common ground for grazing livestock. In 1663 the City Assembly of Dublin decided the land could generate income for the city. It wasn't until Sir Arthur Guinness, later known as Ardilaun, bought the Green in 1877 that the land was redeveloped and opened as St. Stephen's Green to the public on July 27th, 1880.

    "That night the city wore the mask of a capital. The five young men strolled along Stephen’s Green in a faint cloud of aromatic smoke. They talked loudly and gaily and their cloaks dangled from their shoulders. The people made way for them. At the corner of Grafton Street a short fat man was putting two handsome ladies in a car in charge of another fat man. The car drove off and the short fat man caught sight of the party." (p. 46).

  6. 6. Museum of Literature

    If you were able to find the statue of James Joyce, turn around, exit the park and right in front of you, you'll find the entrance to the Museum of Literature Ireland. The Museum of Literature Ireland, celebrates Irish writers featuring not only James Joyce, but Oscar Wilde, Mary Murphy, and more! Visit the permanent exhibit "Dear, Dirty Dublin" a 3D experience of Dublin through Joyce's eyes. Joyce's works are featured throughout the museum, including his early writings and letters to other authors about, "Dubliners". If you need to take a quick break visit the Commons Cafe, which is set in the original student dining hall of UCD Newman House or enjoy the museum's gardens which is home to the protected ash tree that James Joyce had his graduation photo taken.

    Open from 10:30 AM to 5:30 PM. Tickets must be purchased on their website or in-person. First Fridays of the month are free from 6 PM to 9 PM.

    Side quest: As you’re walking towards our final destination on the tour, stop by the Shelbourne Hotel, a historic hotel on the north-side of St. Stephen’s Green. Featured in Dubliners and Ulysses, the building holds a deep historic significance in Irish history. In the 1916, during the Easter Rising, the hotel was occupied by 40 British troops to counter the Irish Citizen Army who had occupied St. Stephen’s Green. In 1992, the Constitution of the Irish Free State was drafted in room 112, which is now known as the Constitution Room.
